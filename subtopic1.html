What is a Stock?

A stock (also known as equity) represents a fractional ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you become a part-owner of that company.

Details

  • Common Stock: Gives voting rights and potential for capital gains and dividends.
  • Preferred Stock: Usually no voting rights, but typically pays fixed dividends and has priority in liquidation.
  • Companies issue stocks to raise capital for expansion, operations, or debt repayment.
  • Stock prices fluctuate based on supply and demand, company performance, and market sentiment.

What is a Stock Market?

A stock market is a place (physical or virtual) where stocks are bought and sold. It facilitates the exchange of ownership between buyers and sellers.

Details

  • Primary Market: Where companies issue new stocks to the public for the first time (IPOs).
  • Secondary Market: Where investors trade existing stocks among themselves (e.g., NYSE, NASDAQ).
  • Stock exchanges provide a regulated and transparent environment for trading.
  • The stock market reflects the overall health and sentiment of the economy.

How Do You Make Money?

Investors can profit from stocks primarily through capital appreciation (selling for more than bought) and dividends (share of company profits).

Details

  • Capital Appreciation: Buy low, sell high. The difference is your profit.
  • Dividends: Regular payments made by a company to its shareholders, usually from its earnings.
  • Some companies reinvest profits instead of paying dividends, aiming for higher capital appreciation.
  • Long-term investing often focuses on both capital appreciation and dividend income.

Understanding Risk

Investing in the stock market involves risk, including the potential loss of principal. Stock prices can go down as well as up.

Details

  • Market Risk: The risk that the entire market or a significant portion of it will decline.
  • Company-Specific Risk: Risks related to a particular company's performance, industry, or management.
  • Liquidity Risk: The risk that an investment cannot be quickly bought or sold without a significant loss in value.
  • Diversification is key to managing risk, but it does not eliminate it.

Market Indexes

Stock market indexes (like S&P 500, Dow Jones, NASDAQ) are statistical measures that represent the performance of a specific segment of the stock market.

Details

  • S&P 500: Tracks 500 large U.S. companies, widely considered a benchmark for the U.S. stock market.
  • Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA): Tracks 30 large, publicly owned companies based in the U.S.
  • NASDAQ Composite:Heavily weighted towards technology and growth companies.
  • Indexes help investors gauge overall market health and compare investment performance.

How to Invest?

To invest in the stock market, you typically need a brokerage account. You can then buy individual stocks or diversified funds like ETFs and mutual funds.

Details

  • Brokerage Account: An account with a financial firm that allows you to buy and sell investments.
  • Individual Stocks: Direct ownership in specific companies. Higher risk/reward.
  • ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds): Baskets of securities that trade like stocks. Offer diversification.
  • Mutual Funds: Professionally managed portfolios of stocks, bonds, or other investments.

Key Terms to Know

Familiarize yourself with common stock market terminology to better understand news, reports, and discussions.

Details

  • Bull Market: A market condition where prices are rising or are expected to rise.
  • Bear Market: A market condition where prices are falling or are expected to fall.
  • Volatility: The degree of variation of a trading price series over time.
  • Portfolio: A collection of investments owned by an individual or institution.
  • Diversification: Spreading investments across various assets to reduce risk.
