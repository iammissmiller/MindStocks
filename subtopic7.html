Basic Financial Terms

Understand the fundamental vocabulary of personal finance and investing.

Assets

Anything of economic value owned by an individual or company that can be converted into cash. Assets can be current (liquid, short-term) or fixed (long-term, illiquid).

Why Assets Matter

Assets are the foundation of wealth. They determine financial stability, creditworthiness, and net worth for individuals and companies.

Key Insight

Assets represent what you own and are critical for financial planning and investment decisions.

Example:

A house, car, bank savings, or stock investments are all considered assets.

Quick Facts
  • Current Assets: Cash, savings, accounts receivable, inventory.
  • Fixed Assets: Property, equipment, long-term investments.
  • Listed on the balance sheet as owned resources.
Types of Assets
  • Liquid Assets – Easily converted into cash (e.g., savings, stocks).
  • Fixed Assets – Long-term and less liquid (e.g., real estate, machinery).
Key Takeaways
  • Assets define ownership and wealth.
  • They impact creditworthiness and borrowing capacity.
  • Essential for calculating net worth.
💸

Liabilities

Financial obligations or debts owed to another party. Liabilities represent what a company or individual owes. They can be current (short-term) or long-term.

Why Liabilities Matter

Liabilities show what must be repaid. They affect cash flow, credit rating, and long-term financial stability.

Key Insight

Liabilities represent what you owe, balancing the ownership of assets with obligations.

Example:

Credit card bills, car loans, or a mortgage are all liabilities.

Quick Facts
  • Current Liabilities: Accounts payable, short-term loans, utility bills.
  • Long-term Liabilities: Mortgages, student loans, bonds payable.
  • Listed on the balance sheet as obligations.
Types of Liabilities
  • Current – Due within a year (e.g., supplier payments, credit card debt).
  • Long-term – Due after a year (e.g., home loan, company bonds).
Key Takeaways
  • Liabilities represent debts or obligations.
  • Managing them is crucial to avoid financial stress.
  • Balance with assets for healthy net worth.
📊

Net Worth

The value of all financial and non-financial assets owned, minus the value of all outstanding liabilities. It's a key indicator of an individual's or company's financial health.

Why Net Worth Matters

Net worth reflects overall financial strength. It shows whether assets are sufficient to cover liabilities, and whether wealth is growing over time.

Key Formula

Assets – Liabilities = Net Worth

Example:

If someone owns ₹20,00,000 in assets (house, savings, investments) and owes ₹8,00,000 in loans, their net worth is ₹12,00,000.

Quick Facts
  • Positive net worth = more assets than debts.
  • Negative net worth = more debts than assets.
  • Tracking helps measure long-term financial growth.
How to Improve
  • Increase savings & investments.
  • Pay down high-interest debts first.
  • Grow assets through education, business, or property.
Key Takeaways
  • Net worth = snapshot of financial health.
  • It balances what you own vs. what you owe.
  • Essential for setting and tracking financial goals.

Interest

The cost of borrowing money or the return on lending money. It is typically expressed as a percentage of the principal amount. Can be simple or compound.

Why Interest Matters

Interest is at the core of all financial transactions involving lending and borrowing. It affects loans, savings accounts, credit cards, and investments.

Types of Interest

Simple Interest: Based only on the principal amount.
Formula: I = P × R × T

Compound Interest: Calculated on both the principal and the accumulated interest ("interest on interest").

Example:

If you invest ₹10,000 at 10% annual simple interest for 3 years: Interest = ₹10,000 × 10% × 3 = ₹3,000.

If compounded annually: Future Value = ₹10,000 × (1 + 0.10)3 = ₹13,310.

Quick Facts
  • Simple interest benefits borrowers in short-term loans.
  • Compound interest grows wealth faster for investors.
  • High interest rates on debt can trap borrowers.
How to Use Interest Wisely
  • Prefer compound interest when saving or investing.
  • Pay off high-interest debts quickly to avoid snowballing costs.
  • Compare rates before borrowing or investing.
Key Takeaways
  • Interest = cost of borrowing or reward for lending.
  • Can be simple (linear) or compound (exponential growth).
  • Understanding interest is essential for financial literacy.

Inflation

The rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises, reducing the purchasing power of money. It erodes the value of savings and impacts investments.

Why Inflation Matters

Inflation affects everyone — from individuals buying daily groceries to governments managing the economy. A moderate inflation rate is normal in a growing economy, but too much (or too little) can be harmful.

Key Facts About Inflation

Measurement: Usually tracked using indices like the Consumer Price Index (CPI).

Impact: High inflation reduces real returns on savings and investments.

Control: Central banks adjust interest rates to keep inflation stable.

Example:

If inflation is 6% annually, something that costs ₹100 today will cost about ₹106 a year later.

Over 10 years, that same item would cost nearly ₹179, meaning money loses almost half its value.

Quick Tips
  • Track inflation-adjusted (real) returns on investments.
  • Fixed deposits lose value during high inflation periods.
  • Assets like gold, equities, and real estate often hedge inflation.
How to Beat Inflation
  • Invest in assets that historically outpace inflation (e.g., equity, REITs, gold).
  • Maintain a balanced portfolio — avoid relying only on cash.
  • Keep an eye on central bank policies.
Key Takeaways
  • Inflation reduces purchasing power over time.
  • Measured by CPI and managed through monetary policy.
  • Invest smartly to preserve and grow wealth above inflation.

Budget

A financial plan that estimates income and expenses over a set period. It helps manage money effectively and achieve financial goals.

Why Budgeting Matters

Budgeting ensures financial discipline by showing how much you earn, spend, and save. A proper budget helps reduce stress, avoid debt, and prepare for emergencies or long-term goals.

Key Facts About Budgeting

Tracking: Shows where your money is going each month.

Saving: Identifies areas to cut unnecessary expenses.

Goal-Oriented: Helps plan for big purchases, investments, or retirement.

Example:

If your monthly income is ₹50,000:

  • ₹25,000 (50%) → Needs (rent, food, bills)
  • ₹15,000 (30%) → Wants (shopping, entertainment)
  • ₹10,000 (20%) → Savings & Investments
This follows the popular 50/30/20 rule.

Quick Tips
  • Always plan your budget before the month begins.
  • Set aside money for emergencies (at least 3–6 months of expenses).
  • Use apps or spreadsheets to track your expenses daily.
Steps to Create a Budget
  • List your income sources.
  • Write down fixed and variable expenses.
  • Assign categories (Needs, Wants, Savings).
  • Review & adjust monthly to stay on track.
Key Takeaways
  • A budget is a financial roadmap to achieve goals.
  • Helps control spending and boosts savings.
  • Regular review keeps your plan effective.

Credit Score

A numerical expression that represents an individual's creditworthiness. Lenders use it to assess the risk of lending money.

What is a Credit Score?

A credit score is a 3-digit number that reflects your borrowing and repayment behavior. It helps banks and lenders decide whether to approve loans, credit cards, or mortgages.

Key Facts

Range: 300 to 850 (FICO model).
Higher Score: Means lower lending risk and better loan terms.
Factors: Payment history, debt levels, credit age, new credit, and credit mix.

Example
  • 750+ → Excellent (easy approvals, best rates)
  • 650–749 → Good (eligible for most loans)
  • 550–649 → Fair (higher interest rates)
  • Below 550 → Poor (loan rejections likely)
Quick Tips
  • Always pay bills and EMIs on time.
  • Keep credit utilization below 30%.
  • Avoid too many loan/credit card applications at once.
  • Check your credit report regularly for errors.
Steps to Improve Your Score
  • Pay dues before the due date.
  • Maintain a healthy credit mix (secured + unsecured loans).
  • Limit new credit inquiries.
  • Keep old accounts open to build credit history.
Key Takeaways
  • A higher credit score = better financial opportunities.
  • Payment history is the most important factor.
  • Healthy credit habits over time build strong scores.

Key Financial Concepts

✅
Income: Money received, especially on a regular basis, for work or through investments.
✅
Expenses: The cost required for something; money spent on something.
✅
Savings: Money set aside for future use, typically in a bank account.
✅
Investment: An asset or item acquired with the goal of generating income or appreciation.
✅
Diversification: Spreading investments to reduce risk.
✅
Risk Tolerance: An investor's willingness to take on financial risk.
✅
Liquidity: The ease with which an asset can be converted into cash.
